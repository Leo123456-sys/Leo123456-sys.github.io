ABOUT RYSENSTEEN

By Leo D'warte Jensen and Victor Steinrud

Rysensteen Gymnasium is a high school in Copenhagen. The school was created in 1881 as Laura Engelhardt's school in Stormgade 16. It moved in 1895 to Rysensteensgade 3 and in 1919 was taken over by the municipality of Copenhagen as Rysensteen Gymnasium. In 1932, the school moved to Tietgensgade. Around the millennium, the school had new premises in the old stables of the brown meat Village for the subjects geography, visual arts, Chemistry, science and physics. In 2002, an extensive rebuilding of the main building was made, including The administration area and school study centres were renovated. In January 2003, a new music room was completed. In 2011, you chose because of Space shortage to rent into the premises of the white meat town, more specifically the pork square, which is a 7-8-minute walk from the high school main building. In the danish school system you pick a few subjects to study on A levels, which means you study them all the three years you go to gymnasium. In Rysensteen gymnasium you can pick between music, socialstudys, science and language.

Rysensteen


Rysensteen focuses on a project, called the Global Citizenship Programme. This means that the region becomes the subject of a professional immersion in both the study and compulsory subjects as well as in the interdisciplinary projects all classes have in the three high school years. We have now acquired partner schools for all 12 classes so that they will be able to collaborate virtually with pupils from the partner schools during their secondary school and also to run a professionally based exchange journey to the destinations concerned.

About the danish family

In danish typical families most families are small. The nuclear family is the center of the social structure. Danish children are raised to be independent from an early age. Most are put in day care centres at about 1 years old. The Danish government subsidizes child care through the local kommunes. Marriage is not a prerequisite to starting a family. Many couples live together without legalizing the arrangement with marriage, as religion doesn't have a huge importance in Denmark. The classic danish family culture revolves heavily around the word “hygge”. Sitting with the whole family on christmas eve, or just eating dinner together with the family.
Hygge is a Danish and Norwegian word with a partially unique definition. Hygge is one in the Danish adjective, meaning cozy, pleasant, friendly and good. In addition, Hygge also has consistent positive-coated connotations such as security, intimate, in a safe Home, nice and trusting. These meanings are often used as a term for typical Danish. The word hygge implies something nice, casual, safe and recognisable. As these words focus on a physical being/condition, while the Danish and Norwegian focus on a psychological state. A period of time where all psychological, and not physical, needs are balanced. Most often, socialising is associated with social interactions.

There is room for grandmother and sometimes great-grandmother. The whole family is gathered on a household.
